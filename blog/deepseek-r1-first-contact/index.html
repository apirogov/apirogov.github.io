Introduction

Just recently I read an amusing post suggesting that AI is getting to a level that it can be of actual value beyond the hype and cheaply produced trash content. Meanwhile, I have been struggling with certain math-related questions in my quite exotic hobby project and looking for a cheap substitute for a “student research assistant”.

Just like everyone else, a few years ago I played around with ChatGPT, and my overall impression back then was that it was already an amazing tool for translations and style transformations, but beyond useless for anything that required actual reasoning. I lost interest and moved on with life.

Just a few weeks ago I give it another shot. Since then, ChatGPT got many new features – now it can access web pages, generate images and even animations, and it has some form of cross-session conceptual memory (unfortunately I could not make it explain to me how that works, though). Most importantly – the current generation of LLM models are much more capable for tasks that require fact-based and logical reasoning, which has been a well-known weak spot for a long time.

I spent multiple hours trying to teach ChatGPT about my project and teaching it to be more inquisitive and reflective, with mixed success. At times, the responses were very useful – it suggested useful search terms I could use to find the information I need, and gave me high-level overviews about areas of math I am not familiar with. LLMs are a great search interface if you don’t know what you are looking for, as it was in my case. However, whenever I got into more in-depth discussions, I was quickly disappointed. It only feels impressive to a novice, but once you know more about a topic, you quickly start to see all the mistakes and flaws.

So this was roughly my experience with AI, until now. Then, DeepSeek happened. First I just acknowledged it, after reading about it on the news (Nvidia crashing, and all that), but did not care enough to check it out – probably another hype. Yesterday I finally did, and I think you should give it a try as well (just make sure to enable the R1 model in the chat, otherwise you will get a different, pretty underwhelming model).

The First Contact

The Challenge

Slightly annoyed about my overall experience with ChatGPT so far, I decided that my time is too valuable for wasting it on trying to train a chatbot like a dog, so if it could not hold a conversation that feels useful to me, it is simply not ready. If it could not give me a convincing response, I will just have to wait a few more years until AI can be of any use for my purposes. So I just asked, without much hand-holding:

I am building 2D geometry on top of cyclotomic rings in order to have a discrete and exact representation of polygons. It allows each polygon to have a canonical sequence of outer angles that describe the outline. Is the idea clear? The representation is easiest explained with an example. I only consider simple polygons that can be built from unit length line segments meeting at an angle that is possible in the ring. Can you infer the rest? To prove your understanding, give me the sequences of kite and dart Penrose tiles and the minimal suitable cyclotomic ring for it.

What happened, and I cannot say it otherwise, simply stunned me.

ChatGPT never shows what is going on inside its “mind”, but DeepSeek R1 does. It was “thinking” for 308 seconds, which means that it was talking to itself, sounding like a (pretty confused) human trying to figure out the correct answer to a difficult question. To save your valuable time, I suggest not reading that stream of consciousness in full. Just skim it (skip to the next section once you have seen enough), and notice how it actually combines idea generation, self-reflection, recognition of dead-ends and contradictions, and backtracking to evaluate different approaches. In other words, it does in fact look like reasoning.

It is running in circles, is repetitive, is often stuck, fails to see how to get out of its own mental knot, but let’s be real – on a more abstract level, that is exactly what we all do as well! I have piles of college blocks and text files with half-working ideas, repetitive and meandering – just like this output. It makes me wonder: Is talking to ourselves in circles while trying to make some progress really all what we truly are? A big persistent state, a small short-term memory and attention window, and a closed feedback loop generating, processing and filtering information?

Obviously, the ML engineers modelled the reasoning process after our own – this is by design and construction, and it is making perfect sense. The whole idea of artificial neural networks and all the previous break-throughs in image processing were similarly inspired by “nature”. The shocking thing is that apparently all these puzzle pieces are in fact truly good ideas to steal and copy, because in the end they actually work.

I’m not qualified to have a strong opinion on this, but I believe that we in fact can learn more about our own cognition and minds by trying to copy the key ideas we can pin down. We will not find our “human soul” in biology, chemistry or physics, not in the neurons and chemical messages and electrons, but in the way all these things interact and form patterns that result in some behavior or outcome. But the study of patterns is what we call mathematics, and the study of information processing is computer science.

I think that the essence of mind is computational and that right now we are getting closer and closer to understanding it, but feel free to make up your own.

Therefore, the final answer is that the minimal cyclotomic ring is the 10th, and the sequences are [3,3,3,1] for the kite and [4,4,3,-1] for the dart. But we need to confirm the outer angles again to ensure the sequences are correct.

For the kite:

  • Outer angles: 108°, 108°, 108°, 36°.

In steps of 36°, that’s 3, 3, 3, 1. Sum 10. Correct.

For the dart:

  • Outer angles: 144°, 144°, 108°, -36°.

In steps of 36°, that’s 4, 4, 3, -1. Sum 10. Correct.

Hence, the minimal cyclotomic ring is the 10th, and the sequences are as above.

DeepSeek R1 (The Final Response)

The idea is clear: using a cyclotomic ring to represent outer angles of polygons as integer multiples of a base angle, ensuring exact and discrete geometry. For Penrose kite and dart tiles:

  • Minimal Cyclotomic Ring: The 10th cyclotomic ring (ℤ[ζ₁₀]), which corresponds to base angles of 36° (2π/10 radians). This ring can encode all angles of the Penrose tiles as integer multiples.

  • Kite Tile Sequence:
    Outer angles: 108°, 108°, 108°, 36° (convex).
    In ℤ[ζ₁₀]: [3, 3, 3, 1] (each 3 = 3×36° = 108°, 1 = 36°).
    Sum: 3+3+3+1 = 10 (ensures total 360°).

  • Dart Tile Sequence:
    Outer angles: 144°, 144°, 108°, -36° (concave turn as negative).
    In ℤ[ζ₁₀]: [4, 4, 3, -1] (4×36° = 144°, -1×36° = -36° for clockwise turn).
    Sum: 4+4+3-1 = 10 (total 360° preserved).

This leverages ℤ[ζ₁₀] to encode angles exactly, supporting both convex and concave turns via positive/negative coefficients.
